The oldest and most unsettling question in philosophy is not about the universe, or God, or even meaning — it is about the questioner. *Who is asking?*

Adi Shankara's Advaita Vedanta offers a radical answer: the self that appears to ask is itself a superimposition on pure, undifferentiated awareness. Strip away the layers — the body, the thoughts, the memory, the sense of "I" — and what remains is not nothing. It is *chit*, pure consciousness, which was never an object and never became one.

This is not mysticism for its own sake. It is a precise philosophical claim: the subject–object split we take for granted is not fundamental. It is a useful cognitive frame, but not the final word on what is.

Vivekananda made this practical. Realizing the self is not a retreat from the world — it is the ground on which all ethical and productive action stands. The person who has glimpsed their own nature acts without the friction of ego, which is the source of most human suffering.

---

I keep returning to this because the alternative is bleak: a world in which the self is reducible to neural firing, in which consciousness is an epiphenomenon of matter, in which the question *who is asking* has no answer except the silence of physics.

I do not find that convincing. And I find the non-dual tradition — not as belief, but as direct investigation — to be the most honest attempt at an answer I have found.
